Mercury is the smallest planet in our solar system, with a diameter of just 3,031 miles (4,880 kilometers). It is the closest planet to the Sun, with an average distance of about 36 million miles (58 million kilometers). Because of its proximity to the Sun, temperatures on Mercury can reach up to 800 degrees Fahrenheit (430 degrees Celsius) during the day, and drop to -290 degrees Fahrenheit (-180 degrees Celsius) at night.

It is the only planet in our solar system known to support life, with a breathable atmosphere and surface water. The average surface temperature on Earth is around 59 degrees Fahrenheit (15 degrees Celsius).

Mars is the fourth planet from the Sun and is known as the "Red Planet" due to its reddish appearance in the night sky. It has a diameter of 4,212 miles (6,779 kilometers) and is home to the largest volcano and deepest canyon in the solar system. Mars has a thin atmosphere and a colder climate than Earth, with average temperatures of -80 degrees Fahrenheit (-62 degrees Celsius).

Neptune is the eighth and farthest planet from the Sun, with a diameter of 30,599 miles (49,244 kilometers). It is an ice giant and is the windiest planet in our solar system, with winds that can reach up to 1,200 miles per hour (2,000 kilometers per hour). Neptune is also home to the Great Dark Spot, a storm that is similar in size to Jupiter's Great Red Spot.

Pluto is a dwarf planet and is located in the Kuiper Belt, a region of the solar system beyond Neptune's orbit. It has a diameter of 1,473 miles (2,377 kilometers) and was downgraded from full planet status in 2006. Pluto has a thin atmosphere and is thought to be composed mainly of rock and ice.
